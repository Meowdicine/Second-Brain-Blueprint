# What's Worth Storing?

In the AI era, a public second brain should not become a random archive. Store what remains useful after search, chat, and one-off retrieval are no longer enough.

## Priority Order

### 1. Unique decisions and lived context

- Goals, tradeoffs, constraints, and why a decision was made
- Lessons from failed attempts, experiments, and execution
- Drafts, outlines, and notes that reflect your own judgment

Why it matters:
This is the layer AI cannot recreate from the open web.

### 2. Source-backed interpretations

- Authoritative source links with short excerpts or summaries
- Your boundary notes: what the source does and does not support
- Applied takeaways for current projects or routines

Why it matters:
This keeps the wiki grounded in evidence instead of memory drift.

### 3. Project context that gets lost easily

- Requirements, assumptions, dependencies, and blockers
- Stakeholder preferences or team-specific constraints
- Open questions that still need validation

Why it matters:
This is usually the first context to disappear during execution.

### 4. Reusable templates and methods

- Writing templates, review checklists, and decision frameworks
- Repeatable workflows discovered through practice
- Method cards stored in `30_Resources/Methods/`

Why it matters:
Reusable procedures turn isolated wins into a durable operating system.

### 5. Synthesized conclusions

- Your conclusion in your own words
- A clear pointer to the source or evidence behind it
- Next actions or maintenance triggers

Why it matters:
A second brain should preserve processed knowledge, not just collected material.

## What Not to Store

- Generic facts that are easy to search again
- Full article dumps without any synthesis
- Files with no clear future use
- Sensitive personal records in the public template

## Quick Rule

If a note does not help future execution, future decisions, or future retrieval, it probably does not belong in the wiki.
